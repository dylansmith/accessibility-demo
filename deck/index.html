Types of disabilities

Types of disability: Visual

  • Blindness, low vision (cataract, glaucoma), colour blindness
  • Seizures triggered by flashing/flickering images
  • 246 million people have low vision, 39 million are blind (2012)
  • Assistive technologies: screen readers, high contrast, screen magnifiers, text zoom
  • Developers: ensure your markup is semantic, accessible and you have text alternatives for visual media

Types of disability: Auditory

  • Partial or total lack of hearing
  • 360 million people worldwide have disabling hearing loss
  • Increasingly important with the growth of video online (subtitles)
  • Assistive technologies: subtitles, sign language (PIP), transcripts, closed captioning, induction loop
  • Developers: very simple to provide transcripts of audio content

Types of disability: Motor

  • Partial or total lack of motor control
  • e.g.: parapreglia, quadruplegia, cerebral palsy, muscular dystrophy, multiple sclerosis, arthritis, motor neuron disease
  • Assistive technologies: mouth stick, head wand, single-switch access, sip and puff switch, oversized trackball mouse adaptive keyboard, eye tracking, voice recognition software
  • Developers: consider size of UI elements, complex interactions requiring particular dexterity, and time-based interactions

Types of disability: Cognitive

  • Complex array of information processing challenges
  • Stats are hard to come by due to varied conditions and diagnoses
  • Think functional rather than clinical: "how does it impact the user?"
  • Clinical: autism, Downs Syndrome, ADD, dyslexia
  • Functional: attention, memory, problem-solving, comprehension (visual, linguistic, numeric)
  • Developers: considerations are many and equally complex

Why should we care anyway?

The carrot

It's the right thing to do

  • Not being accessible is indefensible from an ethical and CSR perspective
  • Commercial benefits too:
    • improves SEO & general usability
    • access to more customers
    • goodwill leads to customer loyalty
    • a11y is a differentiator and a USP for companies like Apple

1 billion people (15%) of the global population is disabled.

110-190 million adults have very significant challenges.

All of this made more important by an aging and increasingly unhealthy population…

The stick

It's the law

  • UK's Equality Act (2010) makes it illegal to discriminate against people with disabilities
  • Usually, companies are sued by bodies such as the RNIB
  • Section 508 takes a procurement approach to enforcement
  • Many governments have comparable legislation
  • Adverse media is a powerful motivating factor

WCAG2: Overview

  • Published and maintained by the W3C's Web Accessibility Initiative (WAI)
  • The "web standards" of a11y
  • Defines requirements in terms of:
    • 4 principles (POUR): perceivable, operable, understandable, robust
    • containing 12 guidelines
    • with 61 success criteria, graded A, AA or AAA
  • If a site meets all AA success criteria, then it's overall compliance is AA

WCAG2: Perceivable

  • Provide text alternatives for non-text content.
  • Provide captions and other alternatives for multimedia.
  • Create content that can be presented in different ways, including by assistive technologies, without losing meaning.
  • Make it easier for users to see and hear content.

WCAG2: Operable

  • Make all functionality available from a keyboard.
  • Give users enough time to read and use content.
  • Do not use content that causes seizures.
  • Help users navigate and find content.

WCAG2: Understandable

  • Make text readable and understandable.
  • Make content appear and operate in predictable ways.
  • Help users avoid and correct mistakes.

WCAG2: Robust

  • Maximize compatibility with current and future user tools.

Section 508

  • Section 508 Amendment to the Rehabilitation Act of 1973
  • A US law requiring federal agencies and any website contracted to or receiving funds from them to make their content and features accessible to the disabled
  • Legal and technical compliance frameworks
  • Revised in 1998 and is 7 years overdue on approval to bring it in-line with WCAG

BS 8878

  • "Web Accessibility - Code of practice BS 8878"
  • an incredibly catchy name for a web accessibility implementation framework by the BSi
  • outlines how business should develop accessible web products
  • focusses on process as much as product/code

WAI-ARIA 1.0

  • ARIA = Accessible Rich Internet Applications
  • Gives developers a way to attach additional semantics to the DOM
  • As a developer, this is your friend

How to build for accessibility

Step 1: Semantics

Clean, semantic markup is good for everyone!

Checklist

  • Avoid "div-itis": use appropriate HTML5 semantic elements + ARIA
  • Get rid of deprecated elements
  • Headings should be correctly nested and have computed text content
  • Tables are for tabular data (…but you know this)
  • Lists must have child list items
  • Ensure the <html lang> attribute is set

Step 2: Navigation

Are you making it easy for people to navigate in a non-linear manner?

Checklist

  • Links must have meaningful computed text, not "click here"
  • Use the <nav> element with aria-label
  • Add role="main" to your primary content container only
  • Add other landmark roles to key components, e.g. search

Step 3: Media

Is your content still complete and understandable without images/media?

Checklist

  • Every image has an alt attribute, no exceptions
  • Ensure content images have meaningful alt text
  • Put decorative images ("furniture") in CSS or set alt=""
  • Images inside links & headings can have empty alt text if their parent has text content
  • Provide transcripts/subtitles/closed captioning for video & audio

Step 4: Design

Keep it simple, clean and flexible.

Checklist

  • Avoid "traffic light" metaphors and relying solely on visual language
  • Check that there is enough contrast in your design
  • Avoid justified text alignment
  • Use em or rem for layout so that it expands with text zoom (layout should still cope at 300%)

Step 5: Get rid of the mouse

Can you navigate the content easily with your keyboard alone?

Checklist

  • No element should have a tabindex greater than 0
  • Do not remove focus outlines
  • Use tabindex="-1" to enable scriptable focus for non-focusable elements, but only direct focus when it makes things easier
  • Avoid (badly written) custom components

Step 6: Test all the things

Can humans and non-humans understand your code?

Checklist

  • Missing closing tags can confuse screen readers
  • Your output should be valid HTML5
  • Use an automated a11y testing tool, e.g. tenon.io
  • Learn how to use a screen reader, and actually use it
    (bonus credit: turn your screen off)
